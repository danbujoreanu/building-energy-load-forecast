### Q1 — AI governance depth
*"Tell me about the most complex AI governance challenge you've had to solve."*

"The most relevant example is from Meta — I led the Global Ads Auction Testing programme. 30-plus stakeholders across Engineering, Sales, Legal, Finance, and Data Science. Ten-million-dollar-plus quarterly budget. Over 110 experiments running per quarter.

The governance problem wasn't technical — it was that decision rights were completely invisible. Every resource allocation became a VP-level negotiation because nobody knew who actually owned which decisions. The friction was real and it was slowing everything down.

What I built was essentially a governance operating model. A RACI matrix that made the implicit explicit — who decided, who was consulted, who was informed at each stage. A quarterly prioritisation sprint with a scoring rubric so resource decisions could be made at the right level, not escalated. And statistical thresholds that killed underpowered experiments before budget was committed, not after.

The result: VP-level friction dropped significantly. The team moved faster on prioritisation decisions — without anyone gaining formal authority over anyone else. The governance worked because it was structurally sound, not because someone forced compliance.

What I took from that: the hardest governance problems are never about the rules themselves. They're about making decision rights visible. Once people know where the boundaries are, most of the friction dissolves on its own."

---

### Q2 — AI technical depth
*"Your MSc research — what was the actual technical decision you made, and why?"*

"The core decision was between deep learning — specifically LSTMs — and tree-based models. The conventional wisdom in energy forecasting was that LSTMs were the state of the art for time-series data. More complex model, better result. That's the assumption I went in with.

I tested eight approaches across 45 buildings using AMR smart meter data. What I found was that a LightGBM model with well-engineered features — time-of-day, day-of-week, lag variables, rolling averages — matched the LSTM on accuracy at under one percent of the compute cost. R-squared of 0.975.

The decision I had to make was: do I report the LSTM as the winner because it's the sophisticated answer, or do I report what the data actually showed? I reported what the data showed. Published through AICS 2025, Springer proceedings.

What that taught me — and this directly shapes how I think about AI governance — is that the fanciest model is usually not the right model. The real work is in data representation. If you don't understand your features, you don't understand your system. And if you don't understand your system, you can't govern it responsibly. That insight is baked into every governance artefact I've built since."

### Q3 — Cross-functional coordination
*"Governance only works if engineers and product teams actually change how they work. How do you make that happen without a mandate?"*

"Mandate gets you compliance on paper. It doesn't get you behaviour change.

At Meta I built a self-serve troubleshooting tool for our support operation. Six thousand monthly active users at peak. Nobody was required to use it — analysts chose to because it was faster than the alternative. That voluntary adoption was the proof point that got the model rolled out across Meta's full support operation — roughly two million cases annually.

The lesson I took: if you want people to follow a governance process, the process has to be less friction than not following it. The moment it becomes bureaucracy — another form to fill, another approval gate — you've lost. People route around it.

In an AI governance context, that means intake systems that are fast and clear. Checklists that are specific enough to be useful. Escalation paths that don't require a lawyer on every decision. The framework has to be designed for the person in a hurry, not the person who already cares.

That's what I'm most interested in building here — governance infrastructure that product and engineering teams actually want to use, because it makes their lives easier, not harder."
